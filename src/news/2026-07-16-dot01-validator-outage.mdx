*tl;dr: dot01 (1ArdZ) was offline for part of one era. a planned edge change on bkk00 (pulling a
BKNIX 10G port and reconfiguring RPKI) re-advertised the full table to the validator host, BIRD
3.2.1 segfaulted under it, and the bird unit had Restart=no, so the node's /32 was unreachable for
about 4 hours. a later remote power-off jammed the BMC and needed physical hands. we kept dot01
producing by moving its session keys to a backup node and later moved them back. Polkadot does not
slash for downtime and no stake was affected, and we have reimbursed the missed rewards to nominators in full.*

---

## the node

dot01 is a Polkadot active-set validator at 0% commission. 399 nominators nominate it (about 3.3M
DOT of nomination intent); for era 2233 Phragmen elected 29 of them, 1,610,072 DOT, alongside our
10,537 DOT self-bond. it runs on our own hardware in Bangkok (bkk12) with its /32 announced over BGP
on AS142108. the host takes two point-to-point route-reflector uplinks, RR1 to the edge router bkk00
over vlan112 and RR2 to bkk20 over vlan212.

## what happened

we made a planned change on bkk00: we pulled ourselves off a BKNIX 10G port and reconfigured RPKI
on the edge. RPKI origin validation lives inside bkk00's route-filter chains, so changing it, on top
of dropping a transit port, made bkk00 re-evaluate and re-advertise a large part of the BGP table to
its route-reflector clients. bkk12 is one of those clients.

we carry the full table on validators on purpose. having every route lets us influence our own path
selection so the node keeps tight connectivity and does not miss votes. that design is not the
problem. the problem is what the daemon did when the full table churned.

**BIRD 3.2.1 on bkk12 segfaulted.** the coredump is unambiguous:

```
Signal: 11 (SEGV)
Timestamp: Thu 2026-07-16 15:43:28 +07
Command Line: /usr/bin/bird     (BIRD 3.2.1)
Size on Disk: 137.8M
```

a 137 MB core is the full routing table sitting in memory at the moment it died. one crash, at
15:43:28, right in the middle of the edge reconvergence.

it should have restarted and did not, because the bird unit on bkk12 was set to `Restart=no`:

```
FragmentPath=/usr/lib/systemd/system/bird.service
Restart=no
```

so a single segfault took the daemon down and left it down. with bird dead, bkk12 stopped announcing
its /32 (160.22.181.12 and 2401:a860:1012::) and the address was unreachable for about 4 hours. the
polkadot process itself never went down. locally the node looked healthy, which is why the failure
was invisible to anything watching the process rather than the route.

recovery made it worse before it made it better. we restarted bird, then power-cycled the host over
IPMI to clear a suspected hang. the power-off jammed the BMC. it would not bring the box back and
could not be recovered over the network, so we opened a datacenter smart-hands request, which took
about an hour. the host came back at 21:47, bird started on boot, and all four RR sessions
re-established.

## failover

we did not wait on the host. a validator's session keys are separate from its stash, so we moved the
signing workload without touching funds.

1. generated new session keys on a backup node with a valid ownership proof
2. submitted set_keys for dot01's stash through its staking proxy, a key restricted to staking calls
   that cannot transfer or unstake
3. the backup started authoring at the next session
4. the primary recovered at 21:47, resynced, and we set the keys back to it

the two nodes never held the same keys, so there was no double-signing exposure.

## why detection took ~4h

we page on validator grade from TurboFlakes ONE-T through Alertmanager to Pushover. it fired, but
late. grade is a session average, so one unreachable node does not crater it. the number decayed
over several sessions before it crossed the alert threshold, which accounts for most of the 4 hours.
we had no alert on BGP session state and no probe checking the /32 from outside our own network, so
the fast, unambiguous signal (bird is dead, the address is unreachable) never paged us.

## impact

Polkadot does not slash for downtime. an offline validator is disabled for the rest of the era
(dot01 sits at validator index 21 in the relay's disabled set for era 2233), earns no points, and
re-enters automatically at the next era. its validate intention and session keys stayed intact
throughout, so it resumes at era 2234 with no action needed. dot01 earned normal points before the
incident, 84,840 in era 2231 and 82,520 in era 2232, and 0 in era 2233. the forgone reward is one
era of its yield, about 110 DOT in total (era 2232 as the reference), which would have gone to the
29 nominators elected to back it that era. no stake was ever touched.

## reimbursement

we run dot01 at 0% commission, and we cover our own mistakes. we reimbursed the full era-2233
reward to every nominator elected to back dot01 that era, out of our own funds. no nominator needs to
do anything: the credit arrives at the same wallet your staking rewards do (your configured payee,
which for some of you is a separate payout account).

how we sized it. Polkadot pays validators by era points, so dot01's reward for an era is the
network's total validator reward that era times its share of the points. dot01 was disabled and
scored 0 in era 2233, so we use era 2232, a normal era for it, as the reference. all three inputs are
on-chain in Asset Hub staking storage:

```
dot01 era reward = ErasValidatorReward(2232) x dot01_points / total_points
                 = 69,215.64 DOT x 82,520 / 51,692,480
                 = 110.49 DOT
```

that is about 2.5% APY on dot01's stake (110.49 x 365 eras / 1,610,072 DOT), in line with the current
network reward rate, so the figure is not inflated. it is the actual DOT minted to validators that
era, not an assumed rate.

with 0% commission nothing comes off the top, so each nominator's share is pro-rata by their era-2233
stake:

```
nominator share = 110.49 DOT x nominator_stake / 1,610,072 DOT
```

the 29 elected nominators sum to **109.77 DOT**. the remaining 0.72 DOT is our own self-bond's share,
which we absorb.

## fixes

- restart hardening on every bird host: a systemd drop-in with `Restart=on-failure`, `RestartSec=2s`,
  and `StartLimitIntervalSec=0`. this fixes bkk12, which had `Restart=no`, and the rest of the fleet,
  whose packaged unit uses `Restart=on-abort` with a start limit that gives up after five crashes in
  ten seconds. bird should self-heal in seconds and keep retrying until the route settles.
- bring bkk12's bird under the managed config. it had drifted to a hand-rolled unit with `Restart=no`
  instead of the generated one, which is how the restart gap existed only there.
- treat the BIRD 3.2.1 segfault as a bug, not just an operational event. we keep the core, will get a
  symbolized backtrace, and will move the validators onto a patched build. a routing daemon must not
  crash on a full-table reload.
- stage edge changes so a full-table re-advertisement cannot hit validator hosts mid-change. RPKI and
  peering changes on bkk00 get gated and damped toward the RR clients.
- add real-time alerts independent of grade: a BGP session-state alert and an off-net reachability
  probe on the /32, so an unrouted validator pages within seconds instead of after grade decays.
- remote power-off is a last resort after this BMC jam. key-rotation failover worked cleanly and is
  the primary tool for keeping the validator producing through a host problem.
- keep a backup node warm and ready to take dot01's keys.

## the networking reality

Polkadot already has a clever mitigation for exactly this. it does not ship a whole proof of validity
from one validator to another. each candidate's data is erasure coded into chunks, one per validator,
and a node that needs to check a candidate reconstructs it by pulling small chunks from many
validators at once, needing only a fraction of them to rebuild the whole. that sharded, many-to-one
fetch is designed so no single link has to carry the full load.

it helps, but it cannot beat physics or peering economics. reconstruction still means opening
connections to validators spread across the world, and the paths we get from Bangkok are only as good
as the transit between here and there. that is where it breaks: ISPs have little commercial incentive
to maintain fast, well-peered global routes, especially into and out of Asia.

and better transit will soften it more, but the honest truth is that the internet is not evenly
global, and running a competitive validator from Bangkok is swimming against that.

## the bigger picture

running validators from Thailand is hard. connectivity out of Bangkok is expensive and dot01 has been
breakeven at best. that is why we run this lean, and part of why an edge change can turn into a night
like this. we are sourcing better upstream transit to harden the path under the validator.

the routing-layer fix is IPv6. today under a fifth of Polkadot validators are reachable over it, so
most inter-validator traffic is forced onto congested IPv4. as the set adopts IPv6 the timed-out
proof fetches ease for everyone, and we have free IPv6 transit negotiated with Hurricane Electric to
benefit early, with less need for the full-table workaround that bit us here.

further out, JAM, the redesign that replaces the current relay-chain validation model, reworks this
whole core-validation and data-availability layer, so this class of problem should ease at the
protocol level rather than needing us to paper over it. it was initially eyed for around Q3 2026,
though the migration timeline has since moved.

the real fix is structural. we are building a staking-as-a-validator liquid staking product, where
dot01's stash moves under the control of a smart contract instead of a single operator's hands. that
